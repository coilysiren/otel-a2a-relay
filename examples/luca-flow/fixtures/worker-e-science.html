Science · The magnetosphere, briefly

How the Northern Lights work

An aurora is the visible part of a much larger system. Here's the long version, told as a story rather than a list.

Every aurora starts at the surface of the Sun. The Sun is constantly losing its outer atmosphere into space - a thin, fast-moving stream of charged particles called the solar wind. On a quiet day the wind is mild. When a sunspot region tears open and ejects a coronal mass, the wind becomes a gust that crosses the 93 million miles to Earth in a day or two.

Earth has a magnetic field. It's generated by molten iron sloshing around in the outer core, and it reaches well past the orbit of the Moon. That field is the planet's first defense against the solar wind. Most of the wind is deflected around the field, the way water flows around a stone. But the field is not symmetric. On the night side it stretches into a long magnetic tail, and the tail occasionally snaps.

When the tail snaps, it dumps a slug of charged particles back toward Earth along the magnetic field lines. The lines converge near the magnetic poles, so the particles funnel down into the upper atmosphere there - between about 60 and 200 miles up. That's where the show happens.

What makes the colors

The particles slam into oxygen and nitrogen molecules in the thin upper air. Each collision excites an electron in the molecule. When the electron drops back to its rest state, it emits a photon at a very specific wavelength. Atomic oxygen at 100 miles up emits green light at 557.7 nanometers - the classic aurora color. Higher up, around 200 miles, the same oxygen emits red. Excited nitrogen ions emit blue and violet.

AURORA's LED stack is calibrated against these emission lines. The green channel hits 557.7 nm. The violet channel sits at 427.8 nm, where the ionized nitrogen molecule emits its strongest band. The pink between them is real, too - it's what your eye sees when both bands fire at once and a thin upper-atmospheric oxygen line at 630 nm joins in.
